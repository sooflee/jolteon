Understanding SPACs

What blank-check companies are, where they came from, what the returns data actually shows, and what predicts a good one.

What is a SPAC?

A SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) is a shell company with no actual business operations. It exists for one purpose: raise money through an IPO, then find a private company to merge with — which takes that private company public without going through a traditional IPO.

People often call them "blank-check companies" because investors hand over money before knowing what business they'll end up owning.

The SPAC lifecycle

  1. IPO The SPAC sells units to the public, almost always at $10. A unit is usually a share plus a fraction of a warrant. The cash goes into a trust account.
  2. The hunt The sponsors — the people who created the SPAC — have a deadline, usually 18–24 months, to find a private company to acquire.
  3. The merger ("de-SPAC") If shareholders approve the deal, the SPAC merges with the target and your SPAC shares become shares of the new public company. Before the vote, you have the right to redeem your shares for roughly the $10 trust value instead of riding along.
  4. Liquidation If no deal happens by the deadline, the trust is returned to shareholders — again, roughly $10 plus interest.

A short history of SPACs

  • 1980s — The blank-check fraud era Blank-check shell companies flourished in the penny-stock world and became a vehicle for pump-and-dump fraud: promoters raised money with no disclosed business plan, then looted or manipulated the shells.
  • 1990–1992 — Rule 419 kills the blank check Congress passed the Penny Stock Reform Act of 1990, and the SEC's Rule 419 imposed escrow and rescission requirements so onerous that blank-check companies were effectively eliminated from the U.S. market.[1]
  • 1993 — The SPAC is invented Investment banker David Nussbaum of GKN Securities engineered a hybrid: a shell that was legally exempt from Rule 419 (because it held over $5 million in assets, its stock wasn't "penny stock") but voluntarily adopted nearly all of Rule 419's investor protections — the trust account, the redemption right, the deadline. He launched thirteen of these in 1993–94; twelve completed acquisitions. The modern SPAC was born.[1]
  • 2004–2007 — The second wave SPAC IPOs grew from 12 in 2004 to 65 in 2007, reaching 26.6% of all U.S. IPOs that year. Deals got institutional in scale — Liberty Acquisition Holdings raised $1.035 billion in December 2007. By late 2007 SPACs had cumulatively raised over $15 billion, with roughly 10% aggregate annualized returns to that point.[1] The 2008 financial crisis then froze the market almost completely.
  • 2010s — Quiet rebuilding The structure steadily improved for investors — most importantly, redemption rights were decoupled from merger votes, so you could vote yes and still take your $10 back. Volume grew slowly through the decade.
  • 2020–2021 — The boom SPACs exploded during the pandemic: roughly 248 SPAC IPOs raised ~$83 billion in 2020, then 613 raised ~$162 billion in 2021 — more than every prior year combined.[7] Celebrity sponsors, pre-revenue EV startups, and meme-stock enthusiasm defined the era. Redemption rates collapsed (investors stopped taking the safe $10 exit), which in hindsight marked the top.[4]
  • 2022–2023 — The bust Rising rates and dilution gravity caught up. SPACs that merged during the bubble were down over 60% on average by the end of 2022;[4] many high-profile de-SPACs (Lordstown Motors, Virgin Orbit) went bankrupt. Hundreds of SPACs liquidated and returned their trusts.
  • January 2024 — The SEC tightens the rules The SEC adopted final SPAC rules requiring expanded disclosure of sponsor compensation, conflicts of interest, and dilution, and stripping away the legal safe harbor that had let de-SPAC targets market rosy revenue projections that traditional IPOs could never use.[8]
  • 2025–2026 — A disciplined revival The market has partially returned at far smaller scale, with more conventional sponsors, better-aligned structures, and less retail froth — what some practitioners call "SPAC 4.0."[9]

What the returns data shows

The academic evidence is unusually clear, and it splits SPAC investing into two very different games. The same vehicle that is a reliably decent trade before the merger is a reliably bad one after it.

Why holding through the merger loses: dilution

The core finding of Klausner and Ohlrogge's A Sober Look at SPACs is that the $10 you see is not the $10 the merged company gets. Between the sponsor's free 20% equity stake (the "promote"), underwriting fees, and warrant overhang, the SPACs in their 2019–20 sample delivered a mean of just $4.10 in net cash per $10 share (median $5.70) by the time the merger closed.[2] Their follow-up study of the bubble cohort found the same structural drain — about 36% of pre-merger equity — and showed that net cash per share predicted the post-merger crash almost one-for-one.[4] Consistent with this, going public via SPAC cost the median company about 15.1% of post-issue market cap, versus 3.2% for a traditional IPO.[3]

Sophisticated investors respond exactly as you'd expect: in the 2019–20 sample the median SPAC saw 73% of public shares redeemed before the merger, and over a third of SPACs had redemptions above 90%.[2] The institutions that buy at the IPO mostly take the $10 and leave; the losses concentrate on whoever stays in — disproportionately retail investors who bought after a deal was announced.

"Can I invest in a profitable SPAC?"

The catch: a SPAC itself is never profitable. It has no revenue, no products, no operations — it's just a pile of cash in a trust. So you can't screen for "profitable SPACs" the way you'd screen for profitable companies.

What you can do depends on the stage at which you invest:

StageWhat you're buying
Pre-merger A bet on the sponsors finding a good deal, with the trust as a floor. Bought at or below trust value, this is the historically profitable strategy — but the profit comes from redeeming or selling at the merger, not from holding through it.
Target announced Now there's an actual business to evaluate — including whether that company is profitable. Historically most SPAC targets have been early-stage and unprofitable; that's often why they chose a SPAC over a traditional IPO's stricter scrutiny.
Post-merger A regular public stock, and normal stock analysis applies — but it carries the dilution baggage above, which is why the average post-merger SPAC underperforms badly.

What predicts whether a SPAC yields good returns

Researchers have tested most of the intuitive candidates. Roughly in order of how strong the evidence is:

  • Net cash per share (dilution) — the strongest predictor. How much real cash survives the promote, fees, warrants, and redemptions, per share. In Klausner and Ohlrogge's regressions, post-merger share value tracks pre-merger net cash per share nearly one-for-one — shareholders bear essentially the entire cost of the structure.[4] A SPAC with low dilution (small promote, few warrants, a big PIPE relative to trust) starts the race much closer to $10.
  • Redemption rate. High redemptions are the market's verdict on the deal: they both signal a weak target and mechanically worsen dilution for whoever remains. Higher redemption ratios predict lower post-merger returns.[3]
  • Deadline pressure. Deals completed close to the SPAC's expiration perform worse — sponsors facing a total loss of their promote will close a mediocre deal rather than no deal.[3]
  • Target fundamentals. Post-merger returns are lower for targets with under $100 million in trailing revenue, and lower still for unprofitable ones.[3] So your instinct about profitability was right — it just applies to the target, not the SPAC.
  • Sponsor quality — real but weaker than it looks. SPACs run by sponsors affiliated with billion-dollar funds or former Fortune 500 executives do better than average and negotiate less dilutive terms — but many still lost money, and once you control for net cash per share, sponsor quality adds little additional predictive power.[2],[4] A famous name does not repeal the arithmetic.

The honest summary: the best-evidenced way to make money in SPACs has been the unglamorous one — buy pre-merger at or below trust value, capture the yield and any deal-announcement pop, and exit via redemption or sale at the merger. For holding through a merger, the checklist is: low dilution, low redemptions, a deal done early rather than at the deadline, a real-revenue profitable target, and a credible sponsor — and even then you're fighting a structure that has historically favored everyone at the table except you.
