What is a SPAC?

A SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) is a shell company with no business operations. It exists to raise money through an IPO and then merge with a private company. The merger takes the private company public without a traditional IPO. People call SPACs "blank-check companies" because investors hand over money before knowing what business they will end up owning.

The thesis of this page: the SPAC structure is legal and fully disclosed, but it benefits every party except the shareholder who holds through the merger. The data supports three conclusions. Pre-merger shares bought at or below trust value have been a good trade (Part 1). Post-merger shares have been a bad one, because dilution removes roughly half the cash behind each $10 share (Parts 1–2). The disclosed numbers separate good deals from bad ones before the merger closes (Parts 3–4).

The lifecycle

StageWhat happensYour position
1 · IPO The SPAC sells units to the public, almost always at $10. A unit is usually a share plus a fraction of a warrant. The cash goes into a trust account. Cash in trust backs your shares at ~$10
2 · The hunt The sponsors, the people who created the SPAC, have a deadline of usually 18–24 months to find a private company to acquire. You hold a claim on the trust plus a free option on the deal
3 · The merger ("de-SPAC") If shareholders approve, the SPAC merges with the target and SPAC shares become shares of the new public company. Before the vote you may redeem for roughly the $10 trust value instead of riding along
4 · Liquidation If no deal happens by the deadline, the trust is returned to shareholders. You get ~$10 back plus interest
Part 1 · The returns evidence

Pre-merger gains, post-merger losses

SPAC returns split into two distinct games. Pre-merger shares have been a reliably decent trade. Post-merger shares have been a reliably bad one. The table shows the evidence.

StrategyReturnEvidence
Buy at IPO, exit at merger or liquidation ("SPAC arbitrage") +23.9%/yr Equally weighted annualized return across 458 SPAC IPOs, 2010–2020. The trust floor meant even the worst SPAC in the sample returned +0.51%/yr.[3]
Hold through the merger, one year −11.3% Average one-year buy-and-hold return on post-merger shares, vs +19.4% for the broad market over the same periods, about 31 points of underperformance.[3]
Hold through merger · 2019–20 cohort, 3 months −14.5% Median post-merger return. Six- and twelve-month figures were progressively worse.[2]
Hold through merger · bubble cohort (Jul 2020–Dec 2021) −62% Average return to December 2022 across 243 mergers, about 26 points worse than comparable traditional IPOs.[4]
Be the sponsor +619% Average sponsor gain of ~$51M per completed deal. Sponsors earned this even on deals where public shareholders lost money.[3]

Why holding through the merger loses: dilution

$4.10
Mean net cash per $10 share delivered at merger in Klausner & Ohlrogge's 2019–20 sample (median $5.70). The rest went to the sponsor promote, fees, and warrants.

A $10 SPAC share does not deliver $10 to the merged company. The sponsor's free 20% equity stake (the "promote"), underwriting fees, and warrant overhang consumed most of it: the SPACs in Klausner and Ohlrogge's 2019–20 sample delivered a mean of $4.10 in net cash per $10 share by the time the merger closed.[2] Their follow-up study of the bubble cohort measured the same drain, about 36% of pre-merger equity, and found that net cash per share predicted the post-merger crash almost one-for-one.[4] The total cost comparison: going public via SPAC cost the median company about 15.1% of post-issue market cap, versus 3.2% for a traditional IPO.[3]

Institutional investors exit before bearing these costs. In the 2019–20 sample the median SPAC saw 73% of public shares redeemed before the merger, and over a third of SPACs had redemptions above 90%.[2] The losses concentrate on whoever stays in, disproportionately retail investors who bought after a deal was announced.

"Can I invest in a profitable SPAC?"

A SPAC is never profitable. It has no revenue, no products, and no operations. It holds cash in a trust. You cannot screen for "profitable SPACs" the way you screen for profitable companies. The question becomes answerable once a target is announced, and then it applies to the target.
StageWhat you're buying
Pre-merger A bet on the sponsors finding a good deal, with the trust as a floor. Bought at or below trust value, this is the historically profitable strategy. The profit comes from redeeming or selling at the merger, not from holding through it.
Target announced An actual business now exists to evaluate, including whether it is profitable. Most SPAC targets have been early-stage and unprofitable. Avoiding a traditional IPO's stricter scrutiny is often why they chose a SPAC.
Post-merger A regular public stock, and normal stock analysis applies. It carries the dilution described above, which is why the average post-merger SPAC underperforms badly.
Part 2 · What predicts returns

The factors, ranked by evidence

Researchers have tested most of the intuitive candidates. Weight is how strongly the factor predicts post-merger returns in the cited studies.

FactorWhat the evidence showsWeight
Net cash per share (dilution) The strongest predictor. Post-merger share value tracks pre-merger net cash per share nearly one-for-one. Shareholders bear essentially the entire cost of the structure. A deal with a small promote, few warrants, and a large PIPE relative to trust keeps more value behind each share.[4] High
Redemption rate Higher redemptions predict lower post-merger returns.[3] They signal a weak target and mechanically worsen dilution for the shareholders who remain. High
Deadline pressure Deals completed close to the SPAC's expiration perform worse. Sponsors facing total loss of their promote will close a mediocre deal rather than no deal.[3] Medium
Target fundamentals Post-merger returns are lower for targets with under $100M in trailing revenue, and lower still for unprofitable ones. Profitability matters; it applies to the target, not the SPAC.[3] Medium
Sponsor quality A weak predictor. Sponsors affiliated with billion-dollar funds or former Fortune 500 executives perform better than average and negotiate less dilutive terms, but many of their SPACs still lost money, and after controlling for net cash per share, sponsor quality adds little predictive power.[2],[4] Low
Summary: the best-evidenced way to make money in SPACs is to buy pre-merger at or below trust value, collect the yield and any deal-announcement gain, and exit by redemption or sale at the merger. Holding through a merger requires all of the following: low dilution, low redemptions, a deal done early in the window, a profitable target with real revenue, and a credible sponsor. Even then, the structure favors the other parties at the table.
Part 3 · The case study

Case study: DraftKings vs Lordstown

Both were hyped 2020 SPAC mergers in hot sectors. One trades around three times its $10 trust value six years later; the other was bankrupt within three years. Every signal that separated them was visible in the announcement-day filings. The tables below score both deals using only information available at announcement.

Timing is the second caveat: Lordstown tripled after its announcement and did not collapse until the Hindenburg report, five months after closing. The checklist identifies what to avoid holding. It does not predict when the price will correct.
Part 4 · Useful or scam?

How to spot a bad one

Outright scams are the rare case. The more common danger is a SPAC that is legal but structurally built to transfer your money to the sponsor. Both have tells. One habit covers nearly everything: read the filings on SEC EDGAR, the S-1 for the SPAC itself and the merger proxy or S-4 once a deal is announced. The proxy contains the honest version of every number in the pitch deck, including the dilution table and the sponsor's compensation. Scams survive on people not reading these.

Actual-fraud tells

Real frauds share recognizable features. Nikola's founder was convicted of fraud after its SPAC merger. Akazoo largely fabricated its user base. The pattern:

TellWhat it looks like
Unverifiable numbers Revenue claims with no audited financials, customers that are "letters of intent" rather than contracts, products that are demos rather than shipping. Nikola famously rolled a truck down a hill for a promo video.
Wild projections Before 2024, de-SPAC targets could legally market projections a traditional IPO could never publish. Five years of 100%+ growth from near-zero revenue was a hallmark of the worst deals. The SEC closed most of this loophole in 2024.[8] Treat any projection-heavy pitch as a flag.
Promotion over disclosure You learned about it from social media hype, a celebrity endorsement, or a YouTube channel rather than its filings. The SEC issued a 2021 investor alert specifically warning against buying SPACs because a celebrity is attached.
Evasive or recycled sponsors Search the sponsor on EDGAR and in news archives. Serial sponsors with prior blown-up deals, regulatory actions, or penny-stock histories are exactly who the 1980s blank-check rules were written for.

Legal-but-bad tells

Most investor losses come from legal structures, not fraud. These are the Part 2 predictors applied as a checklist:

TellWhat to check
Dilution The merger proxy discloses net cash per share: how much money survives the sponsor's free 20% promote, the fees, and the warrants. If net cash is far below $10 and the deal is priced as if it were not, the gap comes out of you.[2],[4]
High redemptions If 80–90% of the institutional money took the $10 exit before the merger, the smart money has voted. Redemption figures are announced before closing.[3]
Deadline desperation A deal struck in the final months before the SPAC expires means the sponsor chose between any deal and losing their entire promote. Their incentive is not your incentive.[3]
A pre-revenue target Returns were lower for targets under $100M in trailing revenue and lower still for unprofitable ones. "We chose a SPAC because a traditional IPO wouldn't take us" is the subtext of many of these deals.[3]
No skin in the game Did the sponsor buy meaningful shares at $10 alongside you? Did a credible PIPE invest at the deal price? Did the sponsor forfeit part of its promote or extend its lockup? Aligned sponsors do these things; extractive ones don't.
When a SPAC is useful: a credible sponsor with relevant operating experience takes a real-revenue company public on disciplined terms, with low dilution and investors who chose to stay in. It becomes a wealth-transfer machine when dilution, desperation, or hype dominates. The redemption right protects you in either case: until the merger closes you can take roughly $10 back, and no legitimate argument for a deal requires giving that up early.

So are SPACs scams, or just inefficient?

SPACs benefit more than one party, so the premise needs one correction. Sponsors averaged +619% per completed deal. IPO-stage funds earned +23.9% per year with a floor under them. Targets received speed, price certainty, and, before 2024, the right to market projections, with the structure's costs shifted off them.[3] The SPAC benefits every party at the table except the shareholder who holds through the merger. Retail investors who bought after a deal announcement disproportionately filled that seat.

The accurate verdict is inefficiency, not fraud. A scam requires deception, and the SPAC's costs are disclosed: the promote, the warrants, and the dilution table are all in the proxy. Klausner and Ohlrogge describe the costs as obscured: legal, printed, and systematically ignored, which is how the market mispriced the structure for a decade.[2] An inefficient structure persists as long as its losing seat keeps being refilled with less-informed money. The 2020–21 bubble was that dynamic at maximum scale. The bust, the redemption snap-back, and the 2024 SEC rules repriced it.

The verdict: the structure is inefficient by design, and individual deals are sometimes scams. Disclosed dilution you did not read is inefficiency. Lordstown-style misrepresentation is fraud, and it gets prosecuted as fraud. The difference is whether your losses came from the proxy you skipped or from a lie.
Part 5 · The history

From penny-stock fraud to "SPAC 4.0"

EraWhat happened
1980s
fraud era		 Blank-check shells flourish in the penny-stock world as vehicles for pump-and-dump fraud. Promoters raise money with no disclosed business plan, then loot or manipulate the shells.
1990–92
Rule 419		 Congress passes the Penny Stock Reform Act of 1990. The SEC's Rule 419 imposes escrow and rescission requirements so onerous that blank-check companies are effectively eliminated from the U.S. market.[1]
1993
the SPAC is born		 Investment banker David Nussbaum of GKN Securities engineers a hybrid. It is legally exempt from Rule 419 because it holds over $5M in assets, so its stock is not "penny stock," and it voluntarily adopts nearly all of the rule's protections: the trust, the redemption right, the deadline. Thirteen launch in 1993–94; twelve complete acquisitions.[1]
2004–07
second wave		 SPAC IPOs grow from 12 in 2004 to 65 in 2007, reaching 26.6% of all U.S. IPOs that year. Deals turn institutional: Liberty Acquisition Holdings raises $1.035B in December 2007. Cumulative money raised passes $15B, with roughly 10% aggregate annualized returns to that point.[1] The 2008 crisis then freezes the market.
2010s
rebuilding		 The structure steadily improves for investors. The key change: redemption rights are decoupled from merger votes, so you can vote yes and still take your $10 back.
2020–21
the boom		 SPACs explode during the pandemic: ~248 IPOs raise ~$83B in 2020, then 613 raise ~$162B in 2021, more than every prior year combined.[7] Celebrity sponsors, pre-revenue EV startups, meme-stock enthusiasm. Redemption rates collapse as investors stop taking the safe $10 exit, which in hindsight marks the top.[4]
2022–23
the bust		 Rising rates and dilution catch up. Bubble-era mergers are down over 60% on average by end-2022.[4] High-profile de-SPACs (Lordstown Motors, Virgin Orbit) go bankrupt. Hundreds of SPACs liquidate and return their trusts.
Jan 2024
SEC rules		 The SEC adopts final SPAC rules: expanded disclosure of sponsor compensation, conflicts, and dilution, and removal of the legal safe harbor that let de-SPAC targets market rosy revenue projections traditional IPOs could never use.[8]
2025–26
"SPAC 4.0"		 A partial revival at far smaller scale, with more conventional sponsors, better-aligned structures, and less retail froth.[9]
$162B
Raised by 613 SPAC IPOs in 2021 alone, more than every prior year in SPAC history combined.
