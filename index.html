Understanding SPACs

A plain-English guide to blank-check companies — what they are, how they work, and what to know before investing.

What is a SPAC?

A SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) is a shell company with no actual business operations. It exists for one purpose: raise money through an IPO, then find a private company to merge with — which takes that private company public without going through a traditional IPO.

People often call them "blank-check companies" because investors hand over money before knowing what business they'll end up owning.

The SPAC lifecycle

  1. IPO The SPAC sells shares to the public, almost always at $10 per unit. The money goes into a trust account.
  2. The hunt The sponsors — the people who created the SPAC — have a deadline, usually 18–24 months, to find a private company to acquire.
  3. The merger ("de-SPAC") If shareholders approve the deal, the SPAC merges with the target and your SPAC shares become shares of the new public company. Before the vote, you have the right to redeem your shares for roughly the $10 trust value instead of riding along.
  4. Liquidation If no deal happens by the deadline, the trust is returned to shareholders — again, roughly $10 plus a bit of interest.

"Can I invest in a profitable SPAC?"

The catch: a SPAC itself is never profitable. It has no revenue, no products, no operations — it's just a pile of cash in a trust. So you can't screen for "profitable SPACs" the way you'd screen for profitable companies.

What you can do depends on the stage at which you invest:

StageWhat you're buying
Pre-merger A bet on the sponsors finding a good deal. The downside is somewhat protected (you can redeem for ~$10), but the upside depends entirely on a deal that doesn't exist yet.
Target announced Now you can actually evaluate the target company's financials — including whether that company is profitable. Historically, most SPAC targets have been early-stage, unprofitable companies; that's often why they chose a SPAC over a traditional IPO, which carries stricter scrutiny.
Post-merger At this point it's just a regular public stock, and normal stock analysis applies.

Things to know before putting money in

  • The track record is rough. The 2020–2021 SPAC boom ended badly — the majority of companies that went public via SPAC traded well below $10 afterward, and a meaningful number went bankrupt (Lordstown Motors, Virgin Orbit, and others).
  • Sponsor incentives are misaligned with yours. Sponsors typically get ~20% of the SPAC's equity nearly free (the "promote"), so they profit even from a mediocre deal. They're motivated to close any deal before the deadline — not necessarily a good one.
  • Dilution. Warrants and the sponsor promote dilute regular shareholders, which is part of why post-merger prices often sag.
  • The redemption right is your safety net. Buying below trust value pre-merger and redeeming is a low-risk arbitrage strategy some funds run, though returns are modest and your money is locked up.

The bottom line

SPACs trade like normal stocks under regular tickers, so anyone with a brokerage account can buy them. Whether you should is a different question — the structural incentives generally favor sponsors over public shareholders. If you do look at one, the most informative moment is after a target is announced: that's when there's an actual business to evaluate.
