What is a SPAC?

A SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) is a shell company with no actual business operations. It exists for one purpose: raise money through an IPO, then find a private company to merge with — which takes that private company public without going through a traditional IPO. People call them "blank-check companies" because investors hand over money before knowing what business they'll end up owning.

The lifecycle

StageWhat happensYour position
1 · IPO The SPAC sells units to the public, almost always at $10 — usually a share plus a fraction of a warrant. The cash goes into a trust account. Cash in trust backs your shares at ~$10
2 · The hunt The sponsors — the people who created the SPAC — have a deadline, usually 18–24 months, to find a private company to acquire. You hold a claim on the trust plus a free option on the deal
3 · The merger ("de-SPAC") If shareholders approve, the SPAC merges with the target and SPAC shares become shares of the new public company. Before the vote you may redeem for roughly the $10 trust value instead of riding along
4 · Liquidation If no deal happens by the deadline, the trust is returned to shareholders. You get ~$10 back plus interest
Part 1 · The history

From penny-stock fraud to "SPAC 4.0"

EraWhat happened
1980s
fraud era		 Blank-check shells flourish in the penny-stock world as vehicles for pump-and-dump fraud: promoters raise money with no disclosed business plan, then loot or manipulate the shells.
1990–92
Rule 419		 Congress passes the Penny Stock Reform Act of 1990; the SEC's Rule 419 imposes escrow and rescission requirements so onerous that blank-check companies are effectively eliminated from the U.S. market.[1]
1993
the SPAC is born		 Investment banker David Nussbaum of GKN Securities engineers a hybrid: legally exempt from Rule 419 (holding over $5M in assets, its stock isn't "penny stock") but voluntarily adopting nearly all of its protections — the trust, the redemption right, the deadline. Thirteen launch in 1993–94; twelve complete acquisitions.[1]
2004–07
second wave		 SPAC IPOs grow from 12 in 2004 to 65 in 2007 — 26.6% of all U.S. IPOs that year. Deals turn institutional: Liberty Acquisition Holdings raises $1.035B in December 2007. Cumulative money raised passes $15B, with roughly 10% aggregate annualized returns to that point.[1] The 2008 crisis then freezes the market.
2010s
rebuilding		 The structure steadily improves for investors — most importantly, redemption rights are decoupled from merger votes, so you can vote yes and still take your $10 back.
2020–21
the boom		 SPACs explode during the pandemic: ~248 IPOs raise ~$83B in 2020, then 613 raise ~$162B in 2021 — more than every prior year combined.[7] Celebrity sponsors, pre-revenue EV startups, meme-stock enthusiasm. Redemption rates collapse (investors stop taking the safe $10 exit), which in hindsight marks the top.[4]
2022–23
the bust		 Rising rates and dilution gravity catch up. Bubble-era mergers are down over 60% on average by end-2022;[4] high-profile de-SPACs (Lordstown Motors, Virgin Orbit) go bankrupt. Hundreds of SPACs liquidate and return their trusts.
Jan 2024
SEC rules		 The SEC adopts final SPAC rules: expanded disclosure of sponsor compensation, conflicts, and dilution, and removal of the legal safe harbor that let de-SPAC targets market rosy revenue projections traditional IPOs could never use.[8]
2025–26
"SPAC 4.0"		 A partial revival at far smaller scale, with more conventional sponsors, better-aligned structures, and less retail froth.[9]
$162B
Raised by 613 SPAC IPOs in 2021 alone — more than every prior year in SPAC history combined.
Part 2 · The returns evidence

Two very different games

The academic evidence is unusually clear, and it splits SPAC investing in two. The same vehicle that is a reliably decent trade before the merger is a reliably bad one after it.

StrategyReturnEvidence
Buy at IPO, exit at merger or liquidation ("SPAC arbitrage") +23.9%/yr Equally weighted annualized return across 458 SPAC IPOs, 2010–2020. The trust floor means even the worst SPAC in the sample returned +0.51%/yr.[3]
Hold through the merger, one year −11.3% Average one-year buy-and-hold return on post-merger shares, vs +19.4% for the broad market over the same periods — ~31 points of underperformance.[3]
Hold through merger · 2019–20 cohort, 3 months −14.5% Median post-merger return; six- and twelve-month figures progressively worse.[2]
Hold through merger · bubble cohort (Jul 2020–Dec 2021) −62% Average return to December 2022 across 243 mergers — ~26 points worse than comparable traditional IPOs.[4]
Be the sponsor +619% Average sponsor gain of ~$51M per completed deal — earned even on deals where public shareholders lost money.[3]

Why holding through the merger loses: dilution

$4.10
Mean net cash per $10 share delivered at merger in Klausner & Ohlrogge's 2019–20 sample (median $5.70) — the rest consumed by the sponsor promote, fees, and warrants.

The core finding of Klausner and Ohlrogge's A Sober Look at SPACs is that the $10 you see is not the $10 the merged company gets. Between the sponsor's free 20% equity stake (the "promote"), underwriting fees, and warrant overhang, the SPACs in their 2019–20 sample delivered a mean of just $4.10 in net cash per $10 share by the time the merger closed.[2] Their follow-up study of the bubble cohort found the same structural drain — about 36% of pre-merger equity — and showed that net cash per share predicted the post-merger crash almost one-for-one.[4] Consistent with this, going public via SPAC cost the median company about 15.1% of post-issue market cap, versus 3.2% for a traditional IPO.[3]

Sophisticated investors respond exactly as you'd expect: in the 2019–20 sample the median SPAC saw 73% of public shares redeemed before the merger, and over a third of SPACs had redemptions above 90%.[2] The institutions that buy at the IPO mostly take the $10 and leave; the losses concentrate on whoever stays in — disproportionately retail investors who bought after a deal was announced.

"Can I invest in a profitable SPAC?"

The catch: a SPAC itself is never profitable. It has no revenue, no products, no operations — it's just a pile of cash in a trust. You can't screen for "profitable SPACs" the way you'd screen for profitable companies. The question only becomes answerable once a target is announced — and then it applies to the target.
StageWhat you're buying
Pre-merger A bet on the sponsors finding a good deal, with the trust as a floor. Bought at or below trust value, this is the historically profitable strategy — but the profit comes from redeeming or selling at the merger, not from holding through it.
Target announced Now there's an actual business to evaluate — including whether it's profitable. Historically most SPAC targets have been early-stage and unprofitable; that's often why they chose a SPAC over a traditional IPO's stricter scrutiny.
Post-merger A regular public stock, and normal stock analysis applies — but it carries the dilution baggage above, which is why the average post-merger SPAC underperforms badly.
Part 3 · What predicts returns

The factors, ranked by evidence

Researchers have tested most of the intuitive candidates. Weight is how strongly the factor predicts post-merger returns in the cited studies.

FactorWhat the evidence showsWeight
Net cash per share (dilution) The strongest predictor. Post-merger share value tracks pre-merger net cash per share nearly one-for-one — shareholders bear essentially the entire cost of the structure. Low dilution (small promote, few warrants, big PIPE relative to trust) starts the race much closer to $10.[4] High
Redemption rate High redemptions are the market's verdict on the deal: they signal a weak target and mechanically worsen dilution for whoever remains. Higher redemptions predict lower post-merger returns.[3] High
Deadline pressure Deals completed close to the SPAC's expiration perform worse — sponsors facing total loss of their promote will close a mediocre deal rather than no deal.[3] Medium
Target fundamentals Post-merger returns are lower for targets with under $100M in trailing revenue, and lower still for unprofitable ones — so the profitability instinct is right; it just applies to the target, not the SPAC.[3] Medium
Sponsor quality Real but weaker than it looks. Sponsors affiliated with billion-dollar funds or former Fortune 500 executives do better than average and negotiate less dilutive terms — but many still lost money, and after controlling for net cash per share, sponsor quality adds little. A famous name does not repeal the arithmetic.[2],[4] Low
The honest summary: the best-evidenced way to make money in SPACs has been the unglamorous one — buy pre-merger at or below trust value, capture the yield and any deal-announcement pop, and exit via redemption or sale at the merger. For holding through a merger, the checklist is: low dilution, low redemptions, a deal done early rather than at the deadline, a real-revenue profitable target, and a credible sponsor — and even then you're fighting a structure that has historically favored everyone at the table except you.
Part 4 · Useful or scam?

How to spot a bad one

Outright scams are the rare case. The more common danger is a SPAC that's perfectly legal but structurally built to transfer your money to the sponsor. Screen for both — they have different tells. The verification habit that covers nearly everything: read the actual filings on SEC EDGAR — the S-1 for the SPAC itself, and the merger proxy/S-4 once a deal is announced. Every number in the pitch deck has a more honest version in the proxy, including the dilution table and the sponsor's compensation. Scams survive on people not reading these.

Actual-fraud tells

Real frauds happen — Nikola's founder was convicted of fraud after its SPAC merger; Akazoo turned out to have largely fabricated its user base — and they tend to share features:

TellWhat it looks like
Unverifiable numbers Revenue claims with no audited financials, customers that are "letters of intent" rather than contracts, products that are demos rather than shipping. Nikola famously rolled a truck down a hill for a promo video.
Wild projections Pre-2024, de-SPAC targets could legally market hockey-stick projections a traditional IPO could never publish — five years of 100%+ growth from near-zero revenue was a hallmark of the worst deals. The SEC closed most of this loophole in 2024,[8] but treat any projection-heavy pitch as a flag.
Promotion over disclosure You learned about it from social media hype, a celebrity endorsement, or a YouTube channel rather than its filings. The SEC issued a 2021 investor alert specifically warning against buying SPACs because a celebrity is attached.
Evasive or recycled sponsors Search the sponsor on EDGAR and in news archives. Serial sponsors with prior blown-up deals, regulatory actions, or penny-stock histories are exactly who the 1980s blank-check rules were written for.

Legal-but-bad tells

This is where most investor losses actually come from — the predictors in Part 3, applied as a checklist:

TellWhat to check
Dilution The merger proxy discloses net cash per share — how much real money survives the sponsor's free 20% promote, the fees, and the warrants. If net cash is far below $10 and the deal is priced as if it weren't, the gap comes out of you.[2],[4]
High redemptions If 80–90% of the institutional money took the $10 exit before the merger, the smart money has voted. Redemption figures are announced before closing.[3]
Deadline desperation A deal struck in the final months before the SPAC expires means the sponsor chose between any deal and losing their entire promote. Their incentive is not your incentive.[3]
A pre-revenue target Returns were lower for targets under $100M in trailing revenue and lower still for unprofitable ones. "We chose a SPAC because a traditional IPO wouldn't take us" is the subtext of many of these deals.[3]
No skin in the game Did the sponsor buy meaningful shares at $10 alongside you? Did a credible PIPE invest at the deal price? Did the sponsor forfeit part of its promote or extend its lockup? Aligned sponsors do these things; extractive ones don't.
The framing to keep: a SPAC is useful when it's genuinely the right tool — a credible sponsor with relevant operating experience taking a real-revenue company public on disciplined terms, with low dilution and investors who chose to stay in. It's a wealth-transfer machine when any of dilution, desperation, or hype dominates. Either way, the redemption right is your personal insurance: until the merger closes, you can always take the ~$10 back, and no legitimate argument for a deal ever requires you to give that up early.
